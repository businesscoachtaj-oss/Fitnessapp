The Basics

Two drinks. One culture.

Kefir is made using kefir grains — a symbiotic matrix of bacteria and yeast held together in a polysaccharide structure called kefiran. They look like small, rubbery cauliflower florets. You add them to milk or sugar water, leave them for 24–48 hours, strain them out, and drink the fermented liquid. The grains go straight back in for the next batch.

They are not actually grains at all — no gluten, no grain. The name refers purely to their appearance. They're a living culture, and like your sourdough starter, they grow over time and last indefinitely.

Type 01

Milk Kefir

Made by fermenting cow's, goat's or sheep's milk with milk kefir grains. The result is a tangy, slightly thick, drinkable cultured dairy product — similar to thin yoghurt but with a far more diverse microbial profile.

The fermentation breaks down lactose (making it more tolerable for many lactose-sensitive people), partially digests the milk proteins, and produces a rich array of LAB and wild yeasts.

Rich, slightly tangy, creamy

Type 02

Water Kefir

Made using water kefir grains (different from milk grains) fermented in sugar water. The result is a light, slightly fizzy, refreshing drink that can be flavoured with fruit, ginger, or citrus in a second fermentation for natural carbonation.

Completely dairy-free. Lower in probiotic diversity than milk kefir but still remarkable — and far more accessible for clients who are vegan or lactose intolerant.

The Science

Why kefir is exceptional

As a PT and nutritionist, this is the part you'll want to understand deeply — because kefir's benefits are unusually well-evidenced compared to most "health" foods.

🦠
Probiotic Diversity

Commercial kefir contains 1–5 strains. Home-fermented kefir from live grains regularly yields 30–50 distinct strains of Lactobacillus, Leuconostoc, Acetobacter, and wild yeasts including Saccharomyces. This diversity is what makes it genuinely superior to any supplement or commercial product.

Probiotic density: 10⁷–10⁹ CFU/ml — comparable to or exceeding high-dose probiotic capsules.
🧬
Gut Barrier & Immunity

Kefiran — the polysaccharide that makes up the grain matrix — is itself bioactive. It crosses into the gut lining and has been shown in studies to modulate immune response, reduce intestinal permeability, and suppress certain inflammatory markers including IL-6 and TNF-α.

Kefiran has demonstrated direct anti-inflammatory effects independent of the probiotic strains it harbours.
🥛
Lactose & Protein

Lactase produced during fermentation breaks down 25–50% of lactose, making milk kefir well-tolerated by most lactose-sensitive individuals. Simultaneously, casein proteins are partially hydrolysed, improving their digestibility and amino acid availability — relevant for your protein-focused clients.

Studies show milk kefir is tolerated by up to 70% of lactose-intolerant individuals who cannot tolerate regular milk.
🧪
Antimicrobial Activity

Kefir produces bacteriocins, hydrogen peroxide, and organic acids that actively inhibit pathogenic bacteria including H. pylori, Salmonella, E. coli, and Staphylococcus aureus. This makes it genuinely useful as a food-based intervention in gut dysbiosis cases.

Kefir's antimicrobial properties have been demonstrated in vitro against 15+ pathogenic species.
💪
Bone Health & Minerals

The fermentation process increases bioavailability of calcium, magnesium, and phosphorus from milk. For a PT context: kefir's combination of highly bioavailable calcium, vitamin K2 (produced by LAB), and magnesium makes it an underrated bone health intervention — particularly relevant for female clients over 35.

Fermentation-enhanced mineral bioavailability has been shown to meaningfully exceed that of unfermented dairy.
🧠
Gut-Brain Axis

Emerging research on the gut-brain axis positions kefir as one of the more interesting dietary tools for mood and cognitive function. The LAB strains in kefir produce GABA precursors, serotonin precursors, and short-chain fatty acids that influence the vagus nerve pathway — a live area of research with meaningful early results.

Several RCTs have shown regular kefir consumption associated with reduced anxiety scores and improved sleep quality.

Kefir vs yoghurt: Yoghurt typically contains 2–7 bacterial strains added after pasteurisation. Home kefir contains 30–50+ live strains that actively colonise and ferment. The matrix structure of kefir grains also means the microorganisms are delivered in a protected, biofilm-like environment that improves survival through stomach acid — giving them a meaningfully better chance of reaching the colon alive.

Pick your starting point. Milk kefir is the richer, more nutritionally dense option. Water kefir is the gateway if you're dairy-free or want something lighter. Both use different grains — you can't substitute one for the other.

1

Add grains to milk

Place 8g of milk kefir grains into a clean glass jar. Pour over 300ml of room-temperature whole milk. This ratio — 8g grains per 300ml milk — is your baseline. Too little milk and the grains ferment it too quickly and over-acidify; too much and it under-ferments. Scale up as your grains grow but always keep the same ratio.

🥛 Why whole milk? Higher fat content feeds the grains better and produces a richer, creamier kefir. You can use semi-skimmed but the grains will be less active over time.
2

Cover loosely and leave at room temperature

Cover the jar with a cloth secured with an elastic band, or a loose lid — never airtight, as CO₂ is produced during fermentation. Leave at room temperature (ideally 20–24°C) for 24–36 hours. A warm kitchen speeds things up; a cool one slows them down.

3

Taste and strain when ready

After 24 hours, taste it. It should be mildly tangy and fairly pourable — 24 hours is actually the thinnest and mildest point, not the thickest. The longer it ferments, the thicker and tangier it becomes as the bacteria progressively coagulate the casein proteins. If it's still very milky and bland, leave another 6–12 hours. Pour through a fine mesh strainer into a clean jar or bottle, stirring gently to help it through.

⏰ Counterintuitively: 24 hrs = thinnest and mildest. 36 hrs = balanced, noticeably thicker. 48 hrs = thick, strongly tangy, least lactose. The bacteria coagulate proteins over time — thickness builds with fermentation, not the other way around.
4

Return grains immediately

The grains left in the strainer go straight back into the jar with fresh milk. That's it — your next batch has already started. The finished kefir goes into the fridge and keeps for 2–3 weeks, though it continues to ferment slowly even cold.

5

Second fermentation (optional but recommended)

For more flavour complexity and natural effervescence: after straining, seal the kefir in a flip-top bottle with a piece of fruit, a strip of lemon peel, or a vanilla pod. Leave at room temperature for 6–12 hours. Open carefully over a sink — it will be fizzy. Refrigerate after.

🫧 The second ferment consumes remaining sugars, increases carbonation, builds flavour, and further reduces lactose. This is where kefir gets genuinely exciting.

1

Make your sugar water

Dissolve 60g plain white sugar in 1 litre of room-temperature filtered water. Stir until fully dissolved. Don't use honey (antimicrobial), coconut sugar (too mineral-heavy initially), or artificial sweeteners. Plain white sugar gives the grains the cleanest fuel.

🌱 The grains consume most of the sugar during fermentation — the finished drink contains very little residual sugar, especially after a longer ferment.
2

Add grains, fruit and minerals

Add 25g water kefir grains to the sugar water. Add 15g of unsulphured dried fruit (apricots, figs, or raisins work well) — these provide minerals the grains need to stay healthy. Add 1g mineral-rich sea salt and a slice of lemon. Cover loosely.

3

Ferment 24–48 hours

Leave at room temperature. After 24 hours you should see bubbles rising — the grains are active. 24 hours gives a lighter, sweeter result. 48 hours gives a tangier, less sweet, more fermented drink. Taste and decide what you prefer.

4

Strain and second ferment

Strain into a clean jar, removing the grains and fruit. Return grains to fresh sugar water immediately. For the finished water kefir: pour into a flip-top bottle with fresh fruit juice, sliced ginger, berries, or citrus. Seal airtight and leave at room temperature 12–24 hours.

🫧 The second ferment is where water kefir transforms — it builds natural carbonation and creates genuinely interesting flavours. Mango and ginger, blueberry and lemon, raspberry and mint all work brilliantly.
5

Refrigerate and enjoy

After the second ferment, refrigerate. Always open over the sink and open slowly — it can be very fizzy. Drink within 1–2 weeks, though it keeps longer. The flavour continues evolving in the fridge.

Keeping your grains alive

Grain care

The grains are the culture — they're what you're protecting and growing. They're remarkably resilient, but a few things will damage or kill them.

🌡️
Temperature

Ideal fermentation temp is 20–26°C. Below 15°C slows them significantly; above 30°C can damage the grain structure over time. Never use boiling or very hot milk — it kills the culture on contact.

🧼
Cleaning

Never use soap or antibacterial products on the strainer or jar you use for grains — residue kills bacteria. Rinse with hot water only. The jar they ferment in doesn't need cleaning between batches.

🚫
Metal

Avoid prolonged contact with metal — reactive metals like copper, zinc, or aluminium can harm the grains. Use glass jars and plastic or wooden spoons. A fine mesh stainless steel strainer used briefly is fine.

🚿
Never rinse the grains

This is the most common beginner mistake. The instinct is to rinse them clean between batches — but you'd be washing off the active microbial community living on the grain surface. Tap water (chlorinated) is particularly damaging. Just strain and return them straight to fresh milk. They self-regulate — no cleaning needed.

🧼
Cleaning your equipment

Never use soap or antibacterial products on anything that touches the grains — residue kills bacteria. Rinse jars and strainers with hot water only. The fermentation jar doesn't need cleaning between batches at all — just pour in the fresh milk and go.

🚫
Metal

Avoid prolonged contact with reactive metals — copper, zinc, or aluminium can harm the grains. Use glass jars and plastic or silicone spoons. A fine stainless steel strainer used briefly is fine — it's extended contact that's the issue.

📈
Growth

Healthy grains grow with every batch — they'll roughly double in volume over a few weeks. When you have too many, give them away, blend into smoothies, or compost. Smaller grains are more active than very large ones — break up big clumps with clean hands.

❄️
Taking a break

Short break (1–2 weeks): submerge grains in fresh milk, seal, refrigerate. Discard that milk on return and start a fresh batch. Long break: freeze grains in a small amount of milk in a zip-lock bag — they'll keep for months and revive with 3–5 fresh batches.

⚠️
Signs of trouble

Healthy grains are white/yellowish, rubbery, and smell pleasantly sour. Discard and start fresh if you see: pink or orange discolouration, black spots, a putrid (not sour) smell, or grains that have gone slimy and lost their shape.

Sharing your culture

Giving grains to someone else

Your grains will outgrow your needs within weeks. Here's how to transfer them cleanly depending on timing and distance.

🤝
Same day / next day

Spoon the grains into a small jar with enough milk to fully submerge them. Seal it. The recipient puts it straight in the fridge or starts a batch immediately. Simplest and best option — no prep needed.

📅
Within a week

Same as above — grains submerged in milk in a sealed jar will keep happily in the fridge for up to a week. Tell the recipient to discard that first batch of milk and start fresh when they're ready. The grains will be fine.

📮
Posting or longer distance

Two methods: Dry: coat grains in powdered milk, spread on baking paper, dry at room temp 24–48 hrs until hard. Bag and send — keeps weeks to months. Recipient rehydrates in fresh milk, changing daily for 3–5 days. Frozen: freeze in a small amount of milk in a zip-lock — keeps months, revives in a few batches.

💬
Tell them this

The first 2–3 batches after any transfer, storage, or revival will likely be weak or slow — that's completely normal, not a sign the grains are dead. Give them a few cycles to settle into their new environment before judging the result.

Milk kefir recipes

Kefir Pancakes

15 MIN · SERVES 2

The acidity of kefir reacts with baking soda to create the fluffiest pancakes imaginable. These are noticeably superior to buttermilk pancakes — lighter crumb, better flavour, and meaningfully more nutritious.

  • Milk kefir240ml
  • Plain flour160g
  • Egg1
  • Butter (melted)30g
  • Sugar15g
  • Baking soda5g
  • Saltpinch
Method
  1. Whisk kefir, egg, butter together. Fold in flour, baking soda, sugar and salt until just combined — lumps are fine. Do not overmix.
  2. Cook in a buttered pan over medium heat. The batter will bubble — flip when bubbles break on the surface, about 2–3 min per side.
kefir-baking soda reaction = exceptional lift

Kefir Marinated Chicken

10 MIN + OVERNIGHT · SERVES 4

The lactic acid in kefir is one of the best natural meat tenderisers available. An overnight kefir marinade produces chicken that is extraordinarily tender, moist all the way through, and deeply flavoured — better than any commercial marinade.

  • Milk kefir300ml
  • Chicken thighs or breast800g
  • Garlic (crushed)4 cloves
  • Lemon zest + juice1 lemon
  • Smoked paprika + cumin3g each
  • Salt + black peppergenerous
  • Fresh herbs (thyme, rosemary)few sprigs
Method
  1. Mix all marinade ingredients. Coat chicken thoroughly, cover, and refrigerate overnight (minimum 4 hrs).
  2. Remove chicken from marinade, shake off excess. Grill, roast at 200°C or pan-sear. The kefir will char slightly on the outside — this is correct and delicious.
  3. Rest 5 min before serving. Internal temperature 74°C+.

Kefir Frozen Yoghurt

10 MIN + FREEZE · SERVES 4

A high-protein, probiotic-rich frozen dessert that tastes genuinely indulgent. Note that freezing reduces (but doesn't eliminate) live bacterial cultures — the nutritional benefits are partially maintained.

  • Milk kefir (full-fat)500ml
  • Raw honey or maple syrup80ml
  • Vanilla extract5g
  • Pinch of salttiny
  • Fruit of choice (optional)200g
Method
  1. Whisk kefir, honey, vanilla and salt together. Fold in fruit if using.
  2. Pour into a shallow tray and freeze 4 hours, scraping with a fork every hour for a granita texture, OR churn in an ice cream maker for 20 min for a creamier result.
  3. Serve straight from the freezer — it softens quickly.

Kefir Cream Cheese

5 MIN + DRAIN OVERNIGHT

Strain your kefir overnight and you get a thick, spreadable cream cheese with a beautiful tang. Higher in protein than commercial cream cheese, with active cultures still present. Use on sourdough, crackers, or as a base for dips.

  • Milk kefir (36–48 hr ferment)500ml
  • Salt3g
  • Fresh herbs / lemon zest (optional)to taste
Method
  1. Line a fine mesh strainer with muslin or a clean cloth over a bowl. Pour in kefir. Cover and refrigerate overnight (8–12 hrs).
  2. The liquid that drains off (whey) is nutritious — save it for smoothies or bread making. The solid left in the cloth is your kefir cream cheese.
  3. Season with salt and stir in herbs or lemon zest. Store in the fridge up to a week.
concentrated protein + active cultures

Kefir Soda Bread

10 MIN + 40 MIN BAKE

No yeast, no starter, no wait. The acidity of kefir activates baking soda for a rapid rise. A dense, tender loaf ready in under an hour — and as with sourdough, the fermented dairy base genuinely improves the nutritional profile over regular bread.

  • Milk kefir350ml
  • Plain flour450g
  • Baking soda5g
  • Salt5g
  • Seeds or oats (optional topping)handful
Method
  1. Mix flour, baking soda and salt. Make a well, pour in kefir. Mix quickly with a fork until it comes together — don't overwork.
  2. Shape into a round on a floured surface. Score a deep cross on top. Scatter seeds or oats if using.
  3. Bake at 200°C for 35–40 min until hollow when tapped underneath. Cool on a rack before cutting.
kefir acidity activates soda — no wait required
Water kefir in the kitchen

Water kefir recipes

Water kefir is primarily a drink — but a spectacularly versatile one. The second fermentation is where the real creativity happens. These recipes are mostly flavour combinations for second-ferment bottles, plus a few culinary uses.

Lemon & Ginger Fizz

2ND FERMENT · 12–18 HRS

The classic water kefir combination. Clean, bright, genuinely refreshing, and naturally more carbonated than any commercial kombucha. This is your baseline second-ferment recipe — nail this and everything else follows.

  • First-ferment water kefir750ml
  • Fresh ginger (sliced thin)3cm piece
  • Lemon juice + zest1 lemon
  • Honey (optional)5g
Method
  1. Add ginger, lemon juice, zest and honey to a flip-top bottle.
  2. Pour in strained first-ferment kefir. Seal tightly. Leave at room temperature 12–18 hours.
  3. Refrigerate. Open very carefully over a sink — burp the cap slowly. Strain out ginger before serving over ice.
natural carbonation + anti-inflammatory ginger

Blueberry & Mint

  • Lime juice½ lime
Method
  1. Lightly muddle blueberries and mint in the bottle. Add lime juice.

Apple & Cinnamon

Cucumber, Lime & Basil

Depends on timing. Same day or next day: spoon grains into a small jar with enough milk to submerge them, seal it, done — they start a batch immediately or refrigerate. Within a week: same method, the grains keep happily in milk in the fridge. Tell them to discard that first batch of milk and start fresh. Posting or longer distance: either dry the grains (coat in powdered milk, air dry 24–48 hrs until hard, bag and send — keeps weeks to months, recipient rehydrates in fresh milk changing daily for 3–5 days) or freeze them in a small amount of milk in a zip-lock bag. Either way, tell the recipient that the first 2–3 batches after transfer will likely be weak — that's completely normal while the grains settle into a new environment.

At room temperature: 24–48 hours is your drinkable window. After 72 hours it becomes very sharp and starts separating into curds and whey — still edible and great for cooking, but not pleasant to drink straight. In the fridge it's a different story — cold dramatically slows fermentation, and grains left submerged in milk in the fridge will produce a milder, slowly fermented kefir over 3–5 days. Beyond 7 days in the fridge the grains start to get hungry and weaken, so refresh them with a new batch regardless of whether you strain it or not.

Sealed in a bottle in the fridge, finished kefir keeps for 2–3 weeks, sometimes longer. The acidity creates a self-preserving environment. It continues to slowly ferment even cold — Days 1–5 is when it's at its freshest and most balanced for drinking straight. Days 5–14 it's tangier and slightly thinner, still excellent in smoothies, marinades and cooking. Days 14–21 it's quite sharp — best used in baking or cooking rather than drinking. Beyond 3 weeks, use your nose: sour is fine, putrid is not. Any discolouration means discard. Practically speaking, with a daily 300ml batch you'll accumulate kefir faster than you can drink it — use the older batches for cooking and the fresh ones for drinking.

Actually the opposite — 24 hours is your thinnest and mildest result. Thickness builds progressively as fermentation time increases, because the bacteria are gradually coagulating the casein proteins in the milk. At 24 hours those proteins are only partially broken down, so the kefir is fairly pourable and mild. By 36 hours it's noticeably thicker and properly tangy — most people's sweet spot. By 48 hours it's at its thickest, strongly sour, and approaching a loose yoghurt consistency. Beyond that it risks separating into curds and whey entirely. So if you want thicker kefir, ferment longer — don't strain earlier. And for even thicker results, strain the finished kefir through muslin in the fridge for a few hours: the whey drains off and you're left with something close to a soft kefir cream cheese.

Almost always under-fermentation — you've strained it too early, your kitchen is too cold, or your grain-to-milk ratio is too low. Remember: 24 hours is the thinnest and mildest point, not the thickest. Leave it a full 36 hours and you'll notice a significant difference in both texture and tang. New grains bought online also take 3–5 batches to fully activate — your first few results will be weak regardless of timing while the culture settles in.

Not ruined — just over-fermented. The liquid is whey and the solids are kefir curds. Both are perfectly edible and nutritious. Whisk them back together or strain the curds to make kefir cream cheese. Start with 100–150ml per day and build up over 1–2 weeks. Introducing large amounts too quickly can cause temporary bloating or digestive changes as your gut microbiome adjusts — this is normal but uncomfortable. Once adapted, 200–300ml daily is a reasonable maintenance dose for most people. For clients with compromised immunity or serious gut conditions, start smaller and advise them to consult their GP.

No — they're distinct cultures adapted to completely different environments. Milk kefir grains are adapted to a protein-rich, lactose-based medium. Water kefir grains are adapted to a sugar-water environment. Putting milk grains in sugar water (or vice versa) will not produce good kefir and will degrade the grains over time.

Pasteurised milk kefir made at home is generally considered safe in pregnancy, and the probiotic benefits are well-studied in this context. However, home-fermented products carry a small theoretical risk if something goes wrong with the fermentation. Advise pregnant clients to discuss with their midwife or GP. Water kefir is lower risk in this regard as there is no dairy involved.

The second fermentation produced more CO₂ than the bottle could handle. This usually happens with very active grains, high-sugar flavourings, or leaving the second ferment too long at warm temperatures. To prevent: burp the bottle (open briefly) after 12 hours, use less high-sugar flavourings, shorten the second ferment, or refrigerate earlier. Always use bottles designed for carbonated drinks — regular glass jars are not pressure-safe.
