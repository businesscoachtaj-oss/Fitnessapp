The Basics

What is lacto-fermentation?

Every vegetable is covered in naturally occurring lactic acid bacteria (LAB) — Lactobacillus and its relatives. Under normal conditions they're dormant. When you submerge vegetables in a salt brine, you create conditions where LAB thrive and everything else dies. They consume the sugars in the vegetables, produce lactic acid, and that acid preserves everything. No vinegar. No heat. No special equipment. The salt creates the conditions; the bacteria do the work.

This is why lacto-fermented food tastes different to vinegar-pickled food — it's genuinely alive. The sourness is biological, not chemical. The complexity of flavour you get from a well-fermented sauerkraut or kimchi comes from weeks of microbial activity producing dozens of organic acids, esters, and flavour compounds.

01 Sauerkraut

Fermented Cabbage

The simplest lacto-ferment. Cabbage + salt, massaged until the cabbage releases its own liquid. Packed into a jar, weighed down, left to ferment. Deep umami, clean acid, utterly versatile.

02 Kimchi

Spiced Fermented Cabbage

Korea's national dish. The same cabbage base as sauerkraut but with gochugaru, garlic, ginger, and spring onion. More complex microbial profile, more bioactive compounds, and one of the most flavourful things you can make at home.

03 Fermented Pickles

Brine-Cured Cucumbers

Not vinegar pickles — genuinely lacto-fermented. Cucumbers submerged in a salt brine with dill and garlic, fermenting for days until they turn from bright green to olive, crunchy and deeply savoury. Incomparably better than shop-bought.

04 Fermented Veg

Carrots & Beyond

Once you understand the brine ratio, you can ferment almost anything — carrots, beetroot, red onion, garlic, chilli, cauliflower, green beans. The technique is identical; the results are endlessly varied.

What brine actually does

Salt does three things simultaneously. First, it draws liquid out of the vegetables through osmosis — creating the brine that submerges them. Second, it selects for LAB — these bacteria are salt-tolerant; most spoilage bacteria and pathogens are not. Third, it firms the texture — salt strengthens pectin in cell walls, keeping fermented veg crunchy rather than mushy. The salt percentage is the single most important variable in the whole process.

The Science

Why lacto-fermented veg is genuinely exceptional

As a PT and nutritionist, this is where lacto-fermented vegetables become professionally relevant. The benefits are distinct from kefir and sourdough — they complement rather than overlap.

🦠
Probiotic Delivery

Lacto-fermented vegetables deliver Lactobacillus strains directly to the gut in a vegetable matrix that protects them through stomach acid better than supplements. Kimchi alone has been shown to contain 10⁸–10⁹ CFU/g — comparable to therapeutic probiotic doses — of diverse Lactobacillus, Leuconostoc, and Weissella strains.

// Distinct from kefir strains — combined use provides broader microbial diversity
🧬
Bioavailability Transformation

Fermentation dramatically increases the bioavailability of minerals in vegetables by breaking down phytic acid and oxalates — the same mechanism as sourdough. Fermented cabbage has significantly higher bioavailable vitamin C, K2, and B vitamins than raw cabbage. The LAB also synthesise folate during fermentation.

// Fermentation-synthesised vitamin K2 is the same form found in grass-fed dairy
🔥
Anti-inflammatory Activity

Multiple RCTs have shown regular kimchi consumption associated with reduced inflammatory markers including CRP, IL-6, and TNF-α. The combination of LAB, capsaicin from gochugaru, allicin from garlic, and glucosinolates from cabbage creates a genuinely multi-mechanism anti-inflammatory effect that no single supplement replicates.

// Particularly relevant for clients with inflammatory conditions or metabolic dysfunction
🧪
Glucosinolate Preservation

Cruciferous vegetables like cabbage contain glucosinolates — precursors to sulforaphane and other potent anti-cancer compounds. Cooking destroys much of the myrosinase enzyme needed to convert them. Lacto-fermentation preserves myrosinase activity, meaning fermented cabbage has superior glucosinolate bioavailability compared to cooked cabbage.

// Raw cabbage has the enzyme but fermentation dramatically improves bioavailability
🩺
Gut Microbiome Diversity

A landmark 2021 Stanford RCT directly compared high-fibre diets versus high-fermented food diets for microbiome diversity. The fermented food group showed significantly greater increases in microbiome diversity and decreases in inflammatory proteins. The effect was stronger than dietary fibre alone — and lacto-fermented vegetables were a primary component.

// Sonnenburg et al., Cell 2021 — one of the most cited microbiome studies of the decade
⚖️
Metabolic Health

Regular kimchi consumption has been associated in multiple studies with improved insulin sensitivity, reduced fasting glucose, and reduced visceral fat accumulation — independent of other dietary factors. The proposed mechanism involves short-chain fatty acid production from fermentation and direct effects of LAB on lipid metabolism and adipogenesis.

// Relevant for weight management and metabolic syndrome clients

Lacto-fermented veg vs supplements: Probiotic supplements typically deliver 1–10 strains in a freeze-dried, often damaged state with poor gastric survival. Lacto-fermented vegetables deliver 10–30+ live, active strains in a food matrix that buffers stomach acid, along with the prebiotic fibre that feeds them, the polyphenols that potentiate their effects, and the minerals and vitamins produced during fermentation. You cannot buy this in a capsule.

The Most Important Variable

Getting salt right

Salt percentage is the single variable that determines whether your ferment succeeds or fails. Too little and spoilage bacteria compete with LAB. Too much and you inhibit even the LAB. Weigh everything — volume measurements are not precise enough here.

Salt percentage is calculated as a ratio of salt to total weight of vegetables (for dry-salted ferments like sauerkraut) or salt to total water weight (for brine ferments like pickles and carrot sticks).

1–1.5% Under-salted

Fast fermentation, mushy texture, higher risk of spoilage. Too low for anything except very short ferments in cool conditions.

2% Sauerkraut & kimchi

The sweet spot for dry-salted cabbage ferments. Enough to select for LAB, slow enough for complex flavour, keeps texture firm.

2–3% Brine ferments

For vegetables submerged in water brine — pickles, carrots, beetroot. 2% for faster/milder, 3% for slower/more complex.

3.5–5% Long/warm ferments

Higher salt for summer ferments, longer fermentation periods, or very firm vegetables. Slows everything down for more control.

5%+ Over-salted

Fermentation stalls. Very salty result. Can be salvaged by rinsing before eating but the probiotic benefit is diminished.

Step by step

How to make all four

Every ferment here follows the same fundamental principle — salt, submerge, wait. The differences are in ratio, timing, and flavouring. Master sauerkraut first and everything else follows naturally.

// On the counter
LOOSE lid only

CO₂ is produced during fermentation and must be able to escape. A cloth, a lid resting on top, or a lid screwed finger-tight only. Never airtight — pressure builds and jars crack or explode.

// In the fridge
SEALED lid

Cold slows fermentation to almost nothing and CO₂ production drops dramatically. Seal it properly once refrigerated to keep everything fresh. This is the only time you seal it airtight.

The one thing that must always be sealed is the vegetables below the brine — not the jar lid. Veg submerged in brine = anaerobic = safe. Veg above the brine line = exposed to air = mould. The weight matters far more than the lid.

Sauerkraut
1–4 weeks · dry-salt method · start here

What you need

Per 1kg cabbage
White or green cabbage1kg
Fine sea salt (non-iodised)20g (2%)
Caraway seeds (optional)5g
Wide-mouth jar (1L+)1
Weight to keep veg submerged1
⚖️ Weigh your salt 20g per 1kg cabbage is your baseline. Never substitute volume measurements — a tablespoon of fine salt weighs very differently to coarse salt.
1

Shred the cabbage finely

Remove the outer leaves and set aside — you'll use one later. Shred the cabbage as finely as you can: 2–3mm strips. A mandoline is fastest but a sharp knife works perfectly. Discard the core.

2

Weigh, salt, and massage

Weigh the shredded cabbage, calculate 2% of that weight, and add that much salt. Toss to combine, then massage firmly with your hands for 5–10 minutes. You're breaking down the cell walls to release the cabbage's own liquid. By the end you should have a significant pool of brine in the bowl.

// The liquid released = the brine that will preserve everything. No added water needed.
3

Pack tightly into a jar

Pack the cabbage into a clean jar in handfuls, pressing firmly down after each addition. You want to eliminate air pockets and force the brine up over the cabbage. Add caraway seeds between layers if using. Pour any brine left in the bowl into the jar.

4

Weigh down and cover

Fold a reserved outer cabbage leaf and tuck it over the top of the shredded cabbage, pushing it down so everything stays submerged. Place a weight on top — a small zip-lock bag filled with 2% brine works well, or a smaller jar filled with water. The cabbage must stay below the brine line at all times.

// Exposure to air = risk of mould on the surface. Submerged = anaerobic = safe fermentation.
5

Ferment at room temperature

Cover the jar with a cloth secured with a rubber band, or rest a lid on top without screwing it down — never airtight. CO₂ produced during fermentation must be able to escape or pressure will build. Leave at room temperature, away from direct sunlight. Check daily: press the cabbage down if it rises above the brine, and skim off any white film on the surface (kahm yeast — harmless, but remove it). Start tasting from Day 5.

6

Taste, then refrigerate when ready

Sauerkraut is ready when it tastes pleasantly sour and complex to you — anywhere from 1 to 4 weeks depending on temperature and preference. Warmer kitchens ferment faster. Once you're happy with it, seal the jar properly and refrigerate — this is the only point you seal it airtight. Fermentation slows dramatically in the cold and it keeps for 6 months or more.

Kimchi
3–7 days + · paste method · most complex flavour

What you need

Per 1 large napa cabbage (~1.2kg)
Napa (Chinese) cabbage1.2kg
Fine sea salt60g
Gochugaru (Korean chilli flakes)40g
Garlic (minced)20g
Fresh ginger (grated)15g
Spring onions (sliced)80g
Fish sauce or soy sauce30ml
Sugar or rice flour paste10g
🌶️ Gochugaru matters Regular chilli flakes are not a substitute — gochugaru has a distinct fruity, smoky heat that is fundamental to kimchi's flavour. Find it in any Asian grocery. For vegan kimchi, replace fish sauce with miso or extra soy.
1

Quarter and salt the cabbage

Cut the cabbage lengthways into quarters, then into roughly 5cm pieces. Toss thoroughly with 60g salt in a large bowl. Leave for 1–2 hours, turning occasionally, until the cabbage has wilted and released significant liquid. Rinse thoroughly under cold water and squeeze out as much liquid as possible. Taste — it should be pleasantly salty, not overpowering.

// This initial salting wilts the cabbage and draws out excess water that would dilute the paste flavour.
2

Make the kimchi paste

Combine gochugaru, minced garlic, grated ginger, fish sauce, and sugar (or a small amount of cooked glutinous rice blended to a paste — this feeds fermentation and improves texture). Mix thoroughly into a vivid red paste. Wear gloves — gochugaru stains everything.

3

Combine everything

Add the squeezed cabbage and sliced spring onions to the paste. Wearing gloves, massage the paste thoroughly into every piece of cabbage — you want complete, even coverage. The mixture should be uniformly vivid red.

4

Pack and ferment

Pack tightly into a clean jar, pressing down firmly to eliminate air pockets. Leave 3–4cm headspace — kimchi expands as it ferments and produces significant CO₂. Rest the lid on top or screw it finger-tight only — not airtight while on the counter. Leave at room temperature for 1–5 days depending on taste preference and temperature.

// Taste from Day 2 — fresh kimchi (geotjeori) is mild and bright. Day 3–5 is where deep fermented complexity develops.
5

Refrigerate and age

Once it reaches your preferred sourness, seal the jar properly and transfer to the fridge — cold is when you can seal it airtight. Unlike sauerkraut, kimchi continues to develop significantly in the fridge over weeks — young kimchi (fresh, bright, crunchy) and aged kimchi (deeply sour, complex, ideal for cooking) are almost two different ingredients. Both are correct. Both are excellent.

Fermented Pickles
3–7 days · wet brine method · use the freshest cucumbers

What you need

Per 1L jar
Small cucumbers (pickling variety)500–600g
Non-chlorinated water600ml
Fine sea salt (2% brine)12g
Fresh dill (stalks and heads)4–5 sprigs
Garlic cloves (whole)4 cloves
Black peppercorns10g
Bay leaves2
Grape leaf or oak leaf (optional)1–2
🥒 Cucumber choice matters Use firm, fresh pickling cucumbers — not the waxy supermarket variety. Slice a small piece off the blossom end (opposite the stem) — this removes enzymes that cause softening. Grape or oak leaves contain tannins that help keep pickles crunchy.
1

Make your brine

Dissolve 12g salt in 600ml of room-temperature filtered or previously boiled water. Never use hot water — it damages the LAB on the cucumbers. Stir until fully dissolved. This is a 2% brine.

2

Prepare and pack the jar

Trim a small slice off the blossom end of each cucumber. Pack the jar with dill, garlic, peppercorns and bay leaves first, then add cucumbers tightly — pack them in vertically, as upright as possible. The tighter the pack the less they float.

// Cucumbers must stay fully submerged. Any part above the brine is exposed to air and will mould.
3

Pour brine, weight, cover

Pour the brine over the cucumbers until fully submerged with at least 2cm brine above them. Add a grape or oak leaf on top if using, then a weight. Rest the lid on top without tightening, or cover with cloth — loose, not sealed. Leave at room temperature away from direct sunlight.

4

Taste from Day 3

The brine will turn from clear to cloudy — this is the LAB working, not spoilage. Start tasting from Day 3. The cucumbers transition from bright green to olive green as they ferment — normal and correct. When they reach the sourness you want, seal the jar properly and refrigerate. The lid stays loose the entire time they're on the counter, sealed only once they go cold.

// Cloudy brine = active fermentation. Clear brine after Day 3 = fermentation may have stalled.

🥕 Use 2% brine for most veg Harder vegetables (beetroot, carrots) can handle 3% for longer ferments. Soft veg (tomatoes, peppers) need 3–3.5% to avoid mushiness.
1

Prepare your vegetables

Cut vegetables into uniform pieces — this ensures even fermentation throughout the jar. Sticks, coins, wedges, or whole small vegetables all work. Uniform size means uniform fermentation time. Harder vegetables ferment more slowly than soft ones — don't mix very hard with very soft in the same jar.

2

Choose your aromatics

This is where you get creative. Garlic and dill is the classic. But also consider: cumin and coriander seeds with carrots, chilli and lime zest with cauliflower, turmeric and black pepper with beetroot, bay and juniper with red onion. The aromatics infuse deeply over the fermentation period.

// The brine becomes the most flavourful thing in the jar — drink it, use it in dressings, add it to soups.
3

Pack, brine, weight, ferment

Pack aromatics into the jar first, then vegetables as tightly as possible — you want minimal brine headroom. Pour 2% brine over everything until fully submerged with 2cm above. Weight down. Rest the lid on top loosely or cover with cloth — never seal airtight while fermenting on the counter. Room temperature, away from sunlight.

4

Taste daily from Day 3

Different vegetables hit their peak at different times. Red onion is often ready in 2–3 days — mild, beautiful pink, excellent on everything. Carrots typically need 4–5 days. Beetroot 5–7 days. Whole garlic cloves can be left for 2–4 weeks. Refrigerate individual vegetables when they reach the flavour and texture you want.

Using your ferments

Recipes for everything you've made

Fermented vegetables are one of the most versatile ingredients in the kitchen. They bring acid, funk, crunch, and depth to almost anything — and unlike most condiments, they're actively improving your gut microbiome while you eat.

Sauerkraut Reuben Toastie

10 MIN · SERVES 1 · Sauerkraut

Sourdough bread, sauerkraut, mature cheese, and grainy mustard. Three fermented components in one sandwich. Add the sauerkraut cold, after toasting — heat destroys the live cultures.

  • Sourdough bread2 thick slices
  • Sauerkraut (cold, straight from jar)60g
  • Mature cheddar or gruyère50g
  • Grainy mustard15g
  • Butter10g
Method
  1. Butter the outside of both bread slices. Spread mustard on the inside of one slice. Add cheese to the inside of both.
  2. Toast in a pan over medium heat, cheese-side down, until golden and melted — 3–4 min per side.
  3. Open the toastie, pile on cold sauerkraut, close and serve immediately.

Sauerkraut & Potato Soup

30 MIN · SERVES 4 · Sauerkraut

A deeply warming Eastern European-style soup. The sauerkraut dissolves into the broth, giving a clean, complex sourness that no amount of lemon juice could replicate.

  • Sauerkraut (with its brine)300g
  • Waxy potatoes (diced)500g
  • Onion (sliced)1 large
  • Smoked paprika5g
  • Good chicken or veg stock1.2L
  • Bay leaves2
Method
  1. Sauté onion in a little oil until soft and golden, about 8 min. Add smoked paprika and stir 1 min.
  2. Add stock, potatoes, sauerkraut, brine and bay leaves. Simmer 20 min until potatoes are tender.
  3. Taste and adjust — the sauerkraut provides both salt and acid so go carefully. Serve with sourdough.
cooking reduces live cultures — still high in bioactive compounds

Kimchi Fried Rice

15 MIN · SERVES 2 · Kimchi

The canonical use for aged kimchi. Use kimchi that's been in the fridge at least 2 weeks — the sourness transforms when it hits a hot pan, caramelising into something extraordinary.

  • Aged kimchi (2+ weeks)200g
  • Cooked rice (day-old, cold)300g
  • Kimchi brine30ml
  • Sesame oil15ml
  • Soy sauce15ml
  • Eggs2
  • Spring onion, sesame seedsto serve
Method
  1. Heat a wok or wide pan until very hot. Add sesame oil, then kimchi. Stir-fry 3–4 min until the kimchi begins to caramelise and catches on the bottom.
  2. Add cold rice. Press flat against the pan, let it sit undisturbed 2 min to crisp, then toss. Add soy sauce and kimchi brine.
  3. Push rice to the side. Fry eggs in the space. Serve topped with egg, spring onion and sesame seeds.
aged kimchi caramelises beautifully — fresh kimchi will not give the same result

Kimchi Pancakes (Kimchijeon)

20 MIN · SERVES 2 · Kimchi

Crispy-edged, chewy-centred savoury pancakes — one of Korea's greatest snacks. Use older, more sour kimchi for the best flavour. Eat with the dipping sauce while hot.

  • Kimchi (roughly chopped)200g
  • Plain flour120g
  • Kimchi brine60ml
  • Cold water80ml
  • Egg1
  • Soy sauce + rice vinegar + sesame oildipping sauce
Method
  1. Mix flour, kimchi brine, cold water and egg into a batter. Fold in chopped kimchi. The batter should be thick.
  2. Pour into a hot, oiled pan to make one large pancake or several small ones. Cook on medium-high 3–4 min until the edges are set and crispy, then flip — 2–3 min the other side.
  3. Mix soy sauce, a splash of rice vinegar and a few drops of sesame oil for the dipping sauce. Serve immediately.
kimchi brine in the batter amplifies flavour throughout

Pickle Brine Potato Salad

20 MIN · SERVES 4 · Fermented Pickles

The brine left in the pickle jar is one of the most underused ingredients in fermented cooking. Tossed through warm potatoes it gives a complex, deeply savoury dressing that no vinegar can match.

  • New potatoes800g
  • Fermented pickle brine80ml
  • Fermented pickles (sliced)100g
  • Dijon mustard15g
  • Olive oil40ml
  • Fresh dill + chives20g each
Method
  1. Boil potatoes until just tender. Drain and immediately toss with pickle brine while still hot — the warm potatoes absorb it deeply.
  2. Whisk mustard with olive oil and another 30ml pickle brine. Pour over potatoes while still warm.
  3. Fold in sliced pickles, dill and chives. Taste — the brine provides most of the salt needed. Serve warm or at room temperature.
warm potatoes absorb brine for maximum flavour penetration

Fermented Pickle Tartare

5 MIN · SERVES 4 · Fermented Pickles

The only tartare sauce worth making. The depth of flavour from a lacto-fermented pickle versus a vinegar pickle is the difference between a good sauce and an extraordinary one.

  • Good mayonnaise150g

  • Pickle brine20ml
  • Capers (roughly chopped)20g
  • Fresh dill + chives10g each
  • Lemon zest½ lemon
Method
  1. Combine all ingredients. Taste and adjust — add more brine for acidity, more mayo for richness.
  2. Refrigerate 30 min before serving — it improves significantly as flavours meld.

Fermented Veg Grain Bowl

Fermented Beetroot Hummus

Blending fermented beetroot into hummus transforms it — deep earthy sweetness, vivid colour, complex acidity. The live cultures partially survive blending. An exceptional dip for sourdough or crudités.

Common questions

Things beginners worry about

Almost certainly kahm yeast — a completely harmless wild yeast that forms a flat, white, sometimes wrinkled film on the surface of ferments. It's not mould (mould is fuzzy and coloured) and it doesn't affect the ferment below. Just skim it off, make sure the vegetables are well submerged, and continue fermenting. It's more common in warmer temperatures or with lower salt percentages. Actual mould — fuzzy, pink, orange, black, or green — means discard the batch.

For dry-salted ferments like sauerkraut: make a small amount of 2% brine (10g salt per 500ml water) and pour in just enough to cover the vegetables. For wet brine ferments: simply make more 2% or 3% brine and top up. The important thing is that no vegetable is exposed to air above the brine line for any extended period. If using sauerkraut, sometimes more vigorous initial massaging solves the problem — the cabbage releases more liquid the longer you work it.

Trust your nose first. Lacto-fermented vegetables smell sour, tangy, pleasantly funky — like a good sour beer or a well-aged cheese. They should never smell rotten, putrid, or like rubbish. Visually: cloudy brine is normal and correct. Fuzzy mould in any colour other than white is a problem — discard the batch. Slimy vegetables that have completely lost their structure are over-fermented past the point of palatability but are not dangerous — use your judgement. When in doubt, throw it out — but with proper salt ratios and submerged vegetables, failure is genuinely rare.

Yes — the brine is arguably more probiotic by volume than the vegetables themselves, since the liquid is dense with LAB. Drink it straight, add it to dressings, use it as a base for sauces, stir it into soups at the end, or take a shot glass of it before meals. Pickle brine is also exceptionally effective for muscle cramps — the electrolytes and acidity trigger a neurological reflex that stops cramping faster than water. Several professional sports teams use it for exactly this purpose.

A long time — months, not weeks. Sauerkraut and kimchi will keep 6 months or more refrigerated. Fermented pickles 2–3 months. Fermented carrots and other hard vegetables 1–3 months. The acidity acts as a natural preservative. They continue to slowly ferment and develop in the fridge — the flavour keeps evolving. The practical limit is usually quality (they may become very sour) rather than safety. Keep them submerged in their brine and they'll last.

Yes — heat above 48°C kills live bacteria. If you cook kimchi into fried rice or sauerkraut into soup, the probiotic benefit is reduced. However, the bioactive compounds, vitamins, minerals, and organic acids largely survive cooking and still provide significant benefit. Think of it as two modes: raw for probiotic benefit (add to grain bowls, sandwiches, eat alongside meals), cooked for flavour (kimchi jjigae, sauerkraut soup, kimchi fried rice). Both are worth doing — they're just doing different things for you.

A few possible causes: your kitchen is too cold (below 18°C slows fermentation significantly — move it somewhere warmer), the initial salt-wilting step didn't draw out enough liquid leaving the paste too concentrated, or the jar is airtight and CO₂ pressure is building. Check the seal is loose enough to burp CO₂. Also check you rinsed the cabbage thoroughly after the initial salting — residual salt inhibits fermentation. If nothing is happening after 3 days, try placing the jar in a slightly warmer spot and leaving it another 2–3 days before giving up.

For most people, yes — and the benefits are well-evidenced. A few specific situations require caution: people with histamine intolerance may react to fermented foods as they're high in histamine; immunocompromised individuals should discuss with their GP before consuming large amounts of unpasteurised fermented food; people with SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth) may find fermented foods worsen symptoms initially. For healthy individuals, start with small amounts (2–3 tablespoons daily) and build up over 1–2 weeks to allow the gut microbiome to adjust without discomfort.
