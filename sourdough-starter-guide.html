A sourdough starter is a living culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria. Mix flour and water, let nature take over, feed it daily — and within a week you have a self-sustaining fermentation engine that will last indefinitely.

The Science

Why sourdough is genuinely different

Long fermentation fundamentally changes the nutritional profile of the bread. Here's what actually happens at a biochemical level.

🦠
Gut Health & Microbiome

During fermentation, lactic acid bacteria produce short-chain fatty acids, organic acids, and bioactive peptides that support gut lining integrity and microbial diversity. The acidic pH also creates a prebiotic-like environment.

LAB fermentation produces butyrate precursors — a key fuel source for colonocytes and gut barrier function.
🧬
Gluten Digestibility

Proteases produced by LAB partially hydrolyse gluten proteins during the long ferment — breaking down the specific peptide sequences most associated with intolerance symptoms. True sourdough is significantly easier to digest than commercial bread.

Studies show up to 50–80% reduction in immunoreactive gluten epitopes after 24hr+ fermentation.
💪
Protein & Amino Acids

Fermentation dramatically increases protein bioavailability by breaking down phytic acid — an antinutrient that binds minerals and inhibits absorption. Phytase activity in sourdough fermentation far exceeds that in commercial yeast breads.

Phytic acid reduction of up to 90% has been observed — improving zinc, magnesium, and iron absorption alongside protein uptake.

On glycaemic index: While not your primary focus — sourdough has a measurably lower GI than conventional bread due to organic acid production slowing starch digestion. Worth knowing for clients tracking blood sugar.

The Method

The recipe

Two ingredients. One jar. Use a kitchen scale — volume measurements introduce too much variability. Hydration ratios matter here.

Ingredients

Lukewarm water (28–32°C)50g

Your starter (kept)25g
Plain or bread flour50g
Lukewarm water50g
🌾 Which flour? Days 1–7: whole wheat or wholemeal plain flour is ideal — the bran carries wild yeast that kickstarts the culture. Once established, switch to plain, bread flour, or wholemeal for daily feeding. Wholemeal plain flour works throughout.
💧 Which water? Never use tap water straight — Australian tap water is chlorinated and will inhibit the wild bacteria and yeast you're cultivating. Use one of these instead:
  • Filtered water — best option
  • Tap water left uncovered overnight — chlorine dissipates in 12–24hrs, free and easy
  • Boiled tap water, fully cooled — boiling drives off chlorine
  • Bottled still water — works perfectly
The overnight method is what most bakers settle into — fill a jug before bed, use it in the morning.
🔄 About the "discard" Each day you keep 25g and split off the rest into a separate lidded container in the fridge. That split-off portion isn't waste — it accumulates as your discard starter, full of flavour and fermentation goodness. See the Discard section below for what to do with it.

Method, step by step

1

Choose your jar

Use a tall, straight-sided jar — 750ml is ideal. Straight sides make it easy to track the rise. Wash well but don't sterilise with bleach; clean, not sterile, is the goal.

2

Day 1 — The initial mix

Combine 50g whole wheat or wholemeal plain flour with 50g lukewarm water. Use filtered water, overnight-rested tap water, or boiled-and-cooled tap water — never straight tap water, as chlorine inhibits the wild yeast and bacteria you're trying to cultivate. Stir vigorously for 1–2 minutes until fully combined. Cover loosely — a cloth, Chux, or slightly ajar lid. Never airtight.

🧬 Why whole wheat or rye? Bran carries more wild yeast and the extra nutrients kickstart microbial activity faster than white flour alone. Wholemeal plain flour works perfectly here.
3

Leave at room temperature

Aim for 21–24°C. Avoid draughts and temperature swings. Mark the jar with a rubber band after mixing to track any rise.

4

Days 2–7 — The daily feeding

Once per day, at roughly the same time: weigh your jar, remove starter until only 25g remains. Transfer the removed portion to a lidded container in the fridge (your discard). Add 50g fresh flour and 50g non-chlorinated water (filtered, overnight-rested, or boiled and cooled) to the jar, stir well, cover loosely, and leave.

🦠 The split is essential — without it, acid concentration builds too high and inhibits yeast activity. Keeping 25g resets the pH and gives the yeast fresh fuel to work with.
5

Watch for signs of life

By Day 3–4: bubbles forming throughout, a slightly sour smell. By Day 5–6: visible rising and falling between feedings. A quiet patch around Day 4 is completely normal — don't quit here.

6

The float test

When your starter reliably doubles within 4–8 hours of feeding and looks domed and bubbly at its peak, drop a teaspoon into a glass of water.

✅ It floats

Your starter is gassy, active, and strong enough to leaven bread. Use it within the next few hours while it's at or just past its peak.

❌ It sinks

Not ready yet. Feed it, mark the jar, and check again in 24 hours. Every starter develops at its own pace.

Life After Week One

After Day 7 — your new routine

This is the part most guides skip. Once your starter is established, everything changes. The daily feeding stops, the fridge becomes your best friend, and you shift to a much more relaxed weekly rhythm.

Days 1–7 · Establishment phase

The old routine

  • 📍Starter lives on the counter at room temp
  • 📅Fed every single day without fail
  • 🌡️Needs warmth to build the yeast colony
  • ⏰You're on its schedule
  • 🔄Discard accumulates daily in fridge
Day 7 onwards · Maintenance phase

Your new routine

  • ❄️Starter lives in the fridge between bakes
  • 📅Fed just once a week (or before baking)
  • 😌You're in control of the schedule
  • 🍞Take out → feed → bake → put back
  • 🔄Discard still goes to the fridge jar as before

The weekly baking cycle

❄️
Monday–Thursday

Starter sits in the fridge. Dormant but alive. You do nothing.

🥄
The night before

Take it out. Keep 25g, move the rest to the discard jar. Add 50g flour + 50g water. Leave on counter.

📈
4–8 hrs later

It peaks — domed, bubbly, doubled. This is when you use it. Do the float test if unsure.

🍞
Bake or back in

Use it for your loaf — or if not baking, put it straight back in the fridge. It'll be ready again next week.

Still out of the fridge after feeding? Yes — always. After you feed it, the starter needs to come to room temperature and reach its peak before you either bake with it or return it to the fridge. Cold starter won't rise dough properly. The fridge is for storage only, not for active fermentation. Think of the fridge as its bed — you wake it up, feed it, let it do its thing, then put it back to sleep.
What if you miss a week? No disaster. Starters kept in the fridge can go 2–3 weeks without feeding. You may see more hooch on top — just pour it off, feed it, and give it an extra feed the next day to perk it back up before baking. Bakers have revived starters left for months.
The Long Game

Will it last? Will it get better?

Yes to both — but the answers are more nuanced than most guides let on. Here's what actually happens to your starter over time.

Will it last forever?

Essentially, yes. A well-maintained starter is genuinely indefinite. The oldest documented starters are over 100 years old — passed between bakers, carried across countries, even dried and revived from powder after months in storage.

The culture reaches a stable microbial equilibrium that becomes increasingly resilient over time. As long as you feed it periodically, it will not die. The only real threats are: prolonged neglect in a warm environment, contamination from a dirty jar, or deliberately throwing it away.

If you ever go away for weeks, simply freeze a tablespoon. Thaw, feed twice, and it'll be back to full strength within 48 hours.

Does it taste better with age?

Yes — but not in a simple linear way, and age alone isn't the main driver. Here's the honest picture:

Weeks 1–2 Flavour is sharp and unpredictable — the microbial community is still settling.
Weeks 3–8 Flavour stabilises. Bread becomes more consistent and reliably sour.
Months 3–6 LAB diversity deepens. More complex, nuanced flavour develops gradually.
Years+ Character is fully established. The starter's flavour profile is genuinely unique to you.

The biggest flavour factors will always be your feeding routine and fermentation time — a well-managed 3-month-old starter beats a neglected 10-year-old every time.

Will I run out of starter when I bake?

This is one of the most common worries — and the answer is no, you will never run out. Here's exactly what happens when you bake:

1❄️Take your starter out of the fridge (your jar has ~125g in it)
2🥄Feed it: keep 25g, discard the rest, add 50g flour + 50g water = 125g again
3📈Wait 4–8 hours for it to peak at room temperature
4🍞Scoop out what your recipe needs — typically 50–100g for a loaf
5❄️The rest (25–75g) goes straight back into the fridge as your maintained starter

Think of it like a bank account — you only ever spend the interest, never the principal. The 25g core culture is always preserved. Even if you accidentally used almost all of it and were left with just a teaspoon, you could feed it and rebuild to a full jar within 24–48 hours. The volume doesn't matter — the culture is what counts.

The only way to truly lose your starter is to leave it completely unfed for months in a warm place, or throw it away on purpose. Neither of which you're going to do.
Know Your Tools

Main starter vs discard — what each one does

These are two different things with two different jobs. Mixing them up is one of the most common beginner mistakes — and it's completely understandable given how most guides explain it.

Main jar

Your active starter

The one you maintain and bake with
  • 🏠Lives in the fridge between bakes
  • 🥄Fed before every bake — 25g kept, fresh flour + water added
  • 📈Left out until it peaks (4–8 hrs) before use
  • 🍞Used to leaven actual sourdough loaves — the yeast is strong enough to make bread rise
  • ⏰Timing matters — use at or just past peak
  • ♾️Never fully used — always 25g kept as the core
vs
Discard jar

Your discard starter

The one you cook with and experiment on
  • 🧊Lives in the fridge permanently — never comes out to "activate"
  • ➕Never fed — just accumulates each day's split-off portion
  • 🥞Used for recipes that don't need a strong rise: pancakes, crackers, flatbreads, pizza
  • 🧪Your low-stakes experiment kitchen — try new recipes here, not with your main starter
  • 📅Use within 1–2 weeks for best flavour
  • 💪Still nutritionally valuable — contains organic acids and partially hydrolysed proteins
So what should you experiment with? The discard, always. It's low stakes — if a recipe doesn't work out, you haven't affected your main starter at all. Once you're comfortable with discard recipes and understand how fermentation affects flavour and texture, then graduate to baking a loaf with the main starter. Think of discard cooking as your training ground.
Customising your loaf

Adding seeds, fruit & more

Timing is everything with additions. Put things in too early and they disrupt fermentation, absorb water from the dough, or tear the gluten structure. The right moment depends on what you're adding — here's exactly when and how.

Addition
When to add
Prep needed?
Notes
Seeds
Final fold
Soak in water 30 min, drain before adding
Dry seeds rob moisture from the dough and create dry pockets. Soaked seeds integrate cleanly and don't compete with hydration.
Oats & grains
Final fold
Soak 30–60 min, drain
Rolled oats and rye flakes soften nicely with a short soak. Whole wheat berries need overnight soaking or they'll stay hard through the bake.
Dried fruit
Final fold
Soak in warm water or tea 20–30 min, drain and pat dry
Dry fruit pulls moisture out mid-bake creating dry pockets around each piece. Soaked fruit integrates seamlessly and won't burn on the crust.
Nuts
Final fold or lamination
None required. Toast lightly for better flavour.
Nuts are forgiving — they don't absorb water so timing is flexible. Toasting first brings out much more flavour in the finished loaf.
Olives
Final fold only
Pat dry thoroughly
The oil and brine will disrupt fermentation if added earlier. Pat them very dry — excess brine weakens the gluten around each olive.
Fresh herbs
Final fold only
None. Use whole leaves or roughly torn.
Herbs release water and oils during bulk fermentation — added early they'll weaken the dough. Rosemary, thyme, and chives all work brilliantly added at the final fold.
Cheese
Final fold or shaping
Cube hard cheese (1–2cm). Grated melts into dough and disappears.
Hard cheeses like cheddar or gruyère work best cubed. Adding at shaping lets you layer it visually — fold cheese into the middle as you pre-shape for pockets throughout.
Spices (cumin, fennel, caraway)
At mixing
None. Add dry.
Dry spices don't affect hydration or fermentation — mix them in with the flour at the start for even distribution through the whole loaf.
Cinnamon
Final fold
None. Use sparingly.
Cinnamon has natural antimicrobial properties — in large amounts it will slow or stall fermentation. Add at the final fold, or do a cinnamon swirl at shaping to keep it away from the active dough as long as possible.
Technique

Lamination — the best way to add bulky ingredients

About halfway through bulk fermentation, wet your hands and the work surface. Gently lift the dough and stretch it outward as thin as you can without tearing — you're aiming for a rough rectangle about the size of an A3 sheet.

Scatter your additions evenly across the entire surface. Then fold the dough back into a ball using a series of envelope folds — left third over centre, right third over, top down, bottom up. Tuck into a tight ball and continue bulk fermentation.

This distributes additions evenly throughout the loaf without tearing the gluten network. Far better than just folding them into the top.

Baking with your active starter

Starter recipes

These all use your active, fed starter at or near its peak. Feed your starter the night before, let it peak, then bake. The loaf comes first — master that, then explore the rest.

The Classic Sourdough Loaf

This is the one. A simple, open-crumbed white sourdough — the foundation recipe everything else builds from. Master this and the rest becomes intuitive.

10 minActive prep
4 hrsBulk ferment
OvernightCold proof
45 minBake
~900gYield
Ingredients
  • Active starter (at peak)100g
  • Bread flour400g
  • Water (lukewarm)300g
  • Fine sea salt9g
Overview

Mix flour + water, rest 30 min (autolyse). Add starter + salt, incorporate well. Perform 4 sets of stretch-and-folds over 2 hours. Bulk ferment until dough is airy and domed (2–4 more hrs). Shape, place in floured banneton, refrigerate overnight. Bake in a Dutch oven at 240°C — 20 min lid on, 25 min lid off.

Full method
1
Autolyse — Mix 400g flour with 280g water (hold 20g back). Stir until no dry flour remains. Cover and rest 30–60 minutes. This hydrates the flour and begins gluten development without any kneading.
💡 The autolyse makes the dough dramatically easier to work with and improves oven spring.
2
Add starter and salt — Add 100g active starter and 9g salt dissolved in the reserved 20g water. Squeeze through your fingers until fully incorporated — about 2–3 minutes. The dough will feel shaggy at first, then come together.
3
Stretch and fold — Every 30 minutes for 2 hours, perform one set of stretch-and-folds: grab one side of the dough, stretch it up and fold it over the centre. Rotate and repeat 4 times. Cover between sets. You'll feel the dough getting stronger and more elastic with each set.
4
Bulk fermentation — Leave the dough covered at room temperature (24°C ideal) for 2–4 more hours after the final fold. It's ready when it's increased by 50–75%, feels airy, and jiggles like jelly when you shake the bowl.
💡 Temperature controls everything here. Warmer = faster. A slow, cool bulk produces better flavour.
5
Shape — Turn the dough onto an unfloured surface. Pre-shape into a rough round, rest 20 min uncovered (bench rest). Then final shape: flip, pull the dough toward you to create surface tension, fold the sides in, roll tightly. Place seam-side-up in a well-floured banneton or bowl lined with a floured cloth.
6
Cold proof — Cover with a shower cap or plastic bag and refrigerate overnight (8–16 hours). The cold slows fermentation and develops flavour. This is also what makes scoring possible — cold dough holds its shape.
7
Bake — Preheat oven to 240°C (220°C fan) with a Dutch oven inside for 45+ minutes. Tip the cold dough directly from the fridge onto baking paper, score with a sharp knife or lame at 45°. Lower into the hot Dutch oven, bake 20 min lid on (creates steam for oven spring), then 20–25 min lid off until deep golden brown.
💡 Don't skip preheating the Dutch oven. The initial steam is what gives sourdough its signature crust and ear.
8
Cool completely — This is the hardest part. Rest on a wire rack for at least 1 hour before cutting. Cutting early lets steam escape and makes the crumb gummy. The crumb continues setting as it cools.

Sourdough Focaccia

The most forgiving sourdough bake you can make. No shaping, no banneton, no scoring. Deeply olive-oiled, dimpled, and endlessly variable with toppings.

3–4 hrsBulk ferment
1–2 hrsPan proof
25 minBake
  • Active starter150g
  • Bread flour500g
  • Water400g
  • Fine sea salt10g
  • Olive oil60ml
  • Flaky salt + rosemaryto top

Mix starter, flour, water and salt. Bulk ferment with 3 sets of stretch-and-folds. Pour half the olive oil into a 30x40cm tray, tip in dough, pour remaining oil over. Stretch to fill the tray. Rest 1–2 hours until puffy. Dimple aggressively with oiled fingers, top generously, bake at 220°C for 22–25 min until deep golden.

Sourdough Pizza Dough

A slow-fermented pizza dough that outperforms any commercial yeast version. Crisp base, complex flavour, and genuinely easy once you've done it once.

Soft, pillowy, slightly enriched rolls with a gentle tang. Made in a pull-apart style — perfect for sharing and far more impressive than they are difficult.

Using your discard starter

Discard recipes

These use your fridge discard — the portion you split off each day. No feeding required, no waiting for a peak. Just take it straight from the fridge and cook. Start here before you attempt a loaf.

🥞

Sourdough Pancakes

15 MIN · SERVES 2–3

The easiest discard recipe there is. Fluffy, tangy, and better than any packet mix. The discard adds a subtle complexity that makes people ask what's different.

  • Discard starter150g
  • Plain flour100g
  • Milk120ml
  • Egg1
  • Baking powder1 tsp
  • Salt + sugarpinch each
  • Butter (for pan)knob
Method
  1. Whisk discard, egg, and milk together until smooth.
  2. Fold in flour, baking powder, salt and sugar — don't overmix, lumps are fine.
  3. Cook in a buttered pan over medium heat, 2–3 min per side until golden.

Seeded Sourdough Crackers

20 MIN + BAKE · MAKES ~40

Thin, crisp, deeply savoury crackers that use up a lot of discard at once. Endlessly customisable with seeds, herbs, and spices. Stores for a week in a tin.

  • Discard starter250g
  • Plain flour50g
  • Olive oil40ml
  • Flaky salt1 tsp
  • Mixed seeds (sesame, linseed, poppy)60g
  • Dried rosemary or za'atar1 tsp
Method
  1. Mix everything into a sticky batter. Spread very thinly on lined baking trays with an offset spatula.
  2. Bake at 180°C for 20–25 min until golden and crisp throughout. Watch the edges.
  3. Break into shards once cooled. Store airtight — they crisp up further as they cool.
↑ zinc + magnesium from seeds and fermentation

Quick Discard Flatbreads

10 MIN · MAKES 4

Ready in under 10 minutes start to finish. The fastest thing you can make with discard. Soft, slightly charred, and perfect alongside anything.

  • Discard starter200g
  • Plain flour100g
  • Olive oil1 tbsp
  • Salt½ tsp
  • Garlic powder (optional)½ tsp
Method
  1. Mix all ingredients into a soft dough. Divide into 4 balls.
  2. Roll each ball thinly on a floured surface.
  3. Cook in a dry, very hot cast iron pan — 1–2 min each side until charred in spots.
  4. Brush with butter or olive oil immediately off the pan.

Sourdough Banana Bread

15 MIN + 55 MIN BAKE

The discard adds a subtle tang that cuts through the sweetness of the banana perfectly. Moister than standard banana bread, and genuinely hard to stop eating.

  • Discard starter120g
  • Very ripe bananas3 large
  • Plain flour200g
  • Butter (melted)80g
  • Eggs2
  • Brown sugar120g
  • Baking soda1 tsp
  • Salt + cinnamon½ tsp each
  • Walnuts (optional)80g
Method
  1. Mash bananas thoroughly. Mix in melted butter, eggs, sugar and discard.
  2. Fold in flour, baking soda, salt and cinnamon until just combined. Fold in walnuts.
